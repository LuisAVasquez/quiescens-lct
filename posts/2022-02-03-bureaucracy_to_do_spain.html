Unlike France, there was not a lot of bureaucracy to do in order to settle in Spain. The main bottleneck came from the lack of understandable instructions to do the bureaucratic procedures.

That meant that figuring out what to do usually took longer than actually doing it. The information on official websites can be confusing and only available in Spanish. We noticed that the easiest way to understand any procedure was to ask someone who had done it before.

Before mentioning all the bureaucracy you have to do to start your time in Donostia, there are some concepts that will be necessary.

Definitions

This is an overview of what you need to know in order to start doing your Spanish bureaucracy.

NIE

The NIE (Número de Identidad de Extranjero) is your Foreigner Identity Number. It will be uniquely linked to you, and you will need it for most bureaucracy.

If you become a tax resident in Spain, the NIE is also your tax system ID (Número de Identificación Fiscal NIF).

The DNI (documento nacional de identidad) is the equivalent for Spanish citizens, so many people use this term. You’ll generally get asked for your “De-ne-ee”: your NIE is the correct response!

TIE

The TIE (Tarjeta de Identidad de Extranjero) is the physical card for your residence permit. It serves as an ID card for foreigners. If you get a new TIE, for example by asking for an extension of your permit, then your new TIE will keep the same NIE.

Empadronamiento

This is simply registering your address with the local town hall. You can do it without a NIE, just using with your passport. You will need a valid proof of accomodation.

Estancia VS Residencia

In most countries, there are only two possible ways in which a foreigner can legally be on their territory: as a tourist, or as a resident. Tourists have very limited rights (for example, they cannot work), and residents have almost the same rights as a citizen (for example, they can use the public health system).

However, Spain has a third option, called Estancia (litterally, “stay”). Estancia is an intermediate status between a tourist and a resident. For example, people with Estancia are allowed to work, but cannot use the public health system.

Why is this important?

  • non-EU foreign students can only get Estancia.
  • EU citizens can get a Residencia just by moving to Spain.
  • In principle, non-EU foreign students can change their Estancia to a Residencia only after finishing their studies.
  • For the purpose of getting Spanish nationality, only the time under Residencia counts, not the time under Estancia.

Digital Certificates

One of the big advantages of Spain is that most common bureacracy can be done online. After you get your first TIE, you can get Digital Certificates, which are used to digitally sign documents and online forms.

The Foreigners Office and The Police Office

In most countries, all bureaucracy concerning foreigners is done at the Foreigners Office (Oficina de Extranjería). However, in Spain, these duties are split between the Foreigners Office and the Police Office (Comisaría de la Policía Nacional).

It is useful to keep this difference in mind, because both offices use the same website to request appointments.

The Police Office is in charge of producing TIEs, while all other bureaucracy is done by the Foreigners Office.

This can lead to weird situations. For example, if you ask for an extension of your permit, and it is approved after the expiration date of your current permit, then you will get a document from the Foreigners Office saying that you are legally allowed to be in Spain, even when you do not have a valid TIE yet (of course, you wwill have to request a new TIE at the Police as soon as possible).

Another weird situation is that there are procedures from the Foreigners Office which ask you to deposit the documents at the Police Office. Then, when you get an answer about your procedure, it will be writen by the Foreigners Office.

Note

The Foreigners Office has a decent digital platform, called MERCURIO, where most bureaucracy can be done online.

The Police Office requires most of it bureaucracy to be done in person.

Bureaucratic culture

Spanish administrative personnel in Donostia tend to be very helpful, even when you do not speak much Spanish or they do not speak much English.

Feel free to ask for clarifications, help filling out forms, and all the instructions you may need.

Even in those cases when booking appointments is necessary, Spanish bureaucrats in Donostia are flexible with time. If you arrive earlier to your appointment, they will take you in, even if you are early by several hours. Arriving later will be a problem if the queues are long, so, do not arrive later than 15 minutes.

If you have very specific issues or a complicated bureaucratic question, it is best for you to go accompanied by a native spanish speaker.

To-do list of bureaucracy

Caution

If you do need at appointment (cita previa) at the Foreigners Office or the Police Office, book it as soon as you arrive to Spain!

Queues are long and some inmigration bureaucracy have deadlines!

For example, if you need a TIE, you must request it during the first 30 days after entering Spain.

Note that getting an appointment counts as starting the request process, so, if your appointment is later than 30 days after entering Spain, you will be ok.

In order to settle in Spain, these are the following steps.

Inmigration bureaucracy

  1. Get a NIE
  2. Get an Empadronamiento
  3. Make sure you have a Right to Stay in Spain
  4. Get a TIE
  5. Get your Digital Certificates
  6. Get your Social Security Number
  7. Optional: Ask for a permit extension
  8. Optional for Year 2 students: Ask for a Job Seeker visa
